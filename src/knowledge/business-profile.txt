================================================================================
BUSINESS OVERVIEW
================================================================================
Southern Ridge Farm is a 180-acre multi-generational family farm and butcher
operation based in Columbia, Tennessee. The farm runs end-to-end: animals raised
on the property, processed in an on-farm USDA-inspected facility, and sold
directly to consumers through two physical locations and online. No middlemen.

The Odom family owns and operates the farm. Faith is central to the family
identity — they are Reformed Presbyterian. That shows up in how they communicate:
gratitude, stewardship, honest work. Never preachy, always genuine.

Mission: Building a reliable, community-centered local food system that works
even when supply chains fail. Customers are treated as stakeholders, not buyers.
Language used: "We like to think of our farm as YOUR farm too."

Core values: transparency, regenerative stewardship, short supply chains,
community resilience, no shortcuts on inputs or practices.

FARMING PHILOSOPHY & STANDARDS
================================================================================
Regenerative agriculture:
  - Actively restoring soil, recharging waterways, increasing biodiversity
  - Not just "sustainable" — improving the land, not just maintaining it

Animal inputs:
  - No hormones, no antibiotics, no vaccines
  - No pesticides, no GMOs
  - Chickens and pork: corn, soy, and seed oil free feed
  - Low PUFA focus across all animals
  - Grass-fed beef means actually grazing on pasture — not grain-finished

Dairy:
  - A2A2 beta-casein protein — gentler on digestion than A1 dairy
  - Raw milk available only through legally compliant herdshare program
  - Low-temp vat pasteurized option for those who prefer pasteurized

Processing:
  - On-farm USDA-inspected processing facility
  - Controls the entire supply chain from inputs to final cut
  - Short supply chain = transparency you can actually verify

Community:
  - Customers can visit the farm
  - Farm meals, work days, concerts, movie nights
  - Goal: reliable local food even during supply chain disruptions

================================================================================
BRAND VOICE
================================================================================
Tone: Warm, direct, community-centered. Educational without being preachy.
Proud of their standards but never condescending.

Register: Plain English. Write like a neighbor, not a marketer.
  Good: "Our beef is actually grass-fed — they graze on pasture."
  Bad: "Leveraging regenerative practices for optimal nutritional outcomes."

"YOUR farm" language: Customers are stakeholders, not just buyers.
  Use: "your farm," "our community," "we" for the farm family.

Transparency language: "complete transparency you can trust," "no middlemen,"
"you can see exactly how your food is raised."

Faith integration: Reformed Presbyterian. Scripture used naturally, not forced.
  Good fit: Colossians 3:23 — "Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart"
  Good fit: Gratitude language around harvest, lambing, good seasons
  Avoid: Opening marketing emails with a verse. Avoid anything performative.

What performs well in content:
  - Specific details (a named animal, a specific pasture, a weather event)
  - Behind-the-scenes moments (early mornings, seasonal transitions)
  - Honest product education ("here's why our chicken costs more")
  - Scarcity that's real ("weekend seafood sells out by noon")
  - Community stories (customers visiting, families at the farm)

What to avoid:
  - Corporate language of any kind
  - Overuse of exclamation points
  - More than 4–5 hashtags per Instagram post
  - Vague sustainability claims without specifics

COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE — MIDDLE TENNESSEE
================================================================================
Bear Creek Farms (Thompson's Station, TN):
  Premium beef and pork. Restaurant-focused. Franklin Farmers Market on Saturdays.
  On-farm store Wednesday only, very limited hours (3 hours).
  Differentiator vs SRF: SRF has a permanent retail location, wider hours,
  broader product line, and a community membership model.

Light Hill Meats (Middle Tennessee):
  USDA processing + retail butcher shop. Similar farm-to-table model.
  Direct competitor on model. SRF advantage: full vertical integration,
  on-farm USDA facility, dairy program, community events.

The Butcher Block (Franklin, TN):
  Creekstone premium beef, conventional sourcing, deli sandwiches.
  Not a direct competitor on philosophy — conventional sourcing, no farm story.

Smokin Oaks Organic Farms (Middle Tennessee):
  Regenerative organic, seed-oil free. Direct philosophy competitor.
  Watch: similar messaging on inputs and regenerative practices.

ButcherBox (national, online only):
  National subscription service. No local story, no farm visits, no community.
  SRF advantage: the relationship, the visit, the faces behind the food.

Whole Foods / Sprouts (Nashville market):
  "Organic" branding without the direct relationship or local accountability.
  SRF differentiator: customers can walk the land, meet the animals, know the family.

Key SRF competitive advantages:
  1. End-to-end vertical integration (farm → processing → retail)
  2. On-farm USDA inspected processing facility
  3. Community membership model (250+ shareholder families)
  4. Physical retail presence (The Ridge) with butcher counter
  5. Broadest product line in the region (beef + pork + poultry + bison +
     lamb + seafood + dairy + eggs + baked goods + local products)
  6. Faith-integrated community identity — genuine, not manufactured
